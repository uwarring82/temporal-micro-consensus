## What this is

The project is preparing the first instance of a new artefact category called a *Harbour View* — an externally-readable, citable presentation of a small framework that proposes classical proper time as emerging by a quantum-Darwinism-style redundancy mechanism. The §1 + §2 draft posted alongside this brief is the first two of five sections.

Before the deeper sections are written, we are looking for one external read by a foundations-metrology physicist who is **outside the Freiburg–NIST orbit** and who has **no prior familiarity with this project's vocabulary**. This is an **open call**: rather than identify and invite a specific reader, the draft and this brief are posted publicly so any qualified reader who would like to contribute feedback can do so directly. The point is not endorsement; the point is to discover, while there is still room to revise, whether the executive overview is readable by an in-audience reader who is meeting the framework for the first time.

Estimated time: **30–60 minutes**, reading + brief written feedback. You do not need to verify any physics claims — every claim in the draft cites the underlying framework note where it is established, and the role of the reader here is presentational-quality review, not technical review.

## What we want to know — four specific questions

1. **Is §1 followable without background?** The executive overview is supposed to be readable by a foundations-metrology physicist who has never seen this project's vocabulary before. Where did you stumble? Was there a sentence that required you to re-read or look something up that the document should have anchored on first use?
2. **Is §2's Claim IV paragraph too dense?** The "operational anchors" paragraph introduces three measures (`I(C:M)`, `F[τ]`, `R_δ`) plus a *resolution anchor* vs *classification anchor* distinction in one breath. If a reader needs a breathing space — for example, a one-sentence concrete example of what "resolution anchor" vs "classification anchor" means in a real clock context — flag it. If the paragraph reads fine, that is also useful to know.
3. **Does the title "Report on Temporal Redundancy and Emergent Proper Time" overclaim?** The artefact-type declaration at the top of the document says the View is a *presentational vantage-point document*, not a peer-reviewed publication. Does the word "Report" in the title nevertheless signal to a naive reader that they should expect a review-article survey? If so, suggest an alternative ("Overview," "Vantage Point," or simply drop the subtitle). If not, please confirm.
4. **Schematic accessibility.** Figure 2's bottom panel uses red (`#a52a2a` outline / `#fce4e4` fill) and green (`#0a7f3f` outline / `#dcfce7` fill) as the principal contrast colours for the anti-redundant vs redundant consensus-state panels; this is the figure most likely to suffer under deuteranopia / protanopia or greyscale rendering, since those two fills collapse to near-identical light greys. Figure 1's Page–Wootters slice highlight is *blue* (`#1f6feb`), reinforced by a thicker stroke and an explicit text label, so its accessibility is better-protected by construction. Please open the SVGs in your browser, take a quick look at greyscale rendering (most browsers offer this in dev-tools), and tell us whether either figure becomes unreadable under colourblindness simulation or in greyscale — particular attention is welcome on Figure 2's bottom panel. We will use shape and label distinctions where colour fails. *(Corrected 2026-05-28: an earlier version of this brief mis-stated that Figure 1 also used red/green; that was a brief-side error, not a figure-side defect.)*

If you notice anything else worth flagging — a stale link, an obvious sentence-level issue, a place where the tone reads more confidently than it should — please mention it. The framework's own anti-claim discipline (the seven anti-claims listed in §2) forbids overstatement; any sentence that reads more confidently than the underlying framework note supports is a defect we want to catch before §§3–5 are written.

## What we are *not* asking

- **Not** a technical verification. Every claim in the draft cites an underlying note that carries the technical content; you do not need to check the physics, the math, or the numerics. The numerics live in a separate toolkit; the worked exemplar is the subject of §4, not yet drafted.
- **Not** an endorsement. The View is issued under Local Stewardship and does not seek external endorsement; the External Constraints anchored in the body (quantum mechanics, special relativity, weak-field general relativity, and the Coastline's other constraint-only references) are cited as constraints, not as parity claims.
- **Not** a re-architecture. The five-section structure, the figure plan, and the seven anti-claims are locked at this stage. Your feedback is admissible for *section-level* revision (a paragraph that needs to be split, a missing definition, a sentence that overclaims) but not for restructuring the document.

If you identify a defect that genuinely requires section-level restructuring, flag it — there is a registered project rule (Scout EC-5) that pilot-reader feedback is an admissible trigger for that. But please flag it rather than redrafting yourself.

## How feedback will be incorporated

- **Acknowledgement.** Readers whose feedback is folded into the released View will be acknowledged by name in the View's reference apparatus, separately from the citations, if they wish. (Acknowledgement is opt-in; readers who prefer to remain unnamed are welcome to say so and will be cited as "an external pilot reader" instead.)
- **Folding pass.** Section-level feedback that requires TOC revision is handled per Scout EC-5 (steward + Guardian sign-off before any structural change). Within-section feedback is folded directly into §1 and/or §2 at the next pre-§§3–5 commit, with the change recorded in the project logbook.
- **Disagreement.** If the steward disagrees with a piece of feedback and decides not to fold it, the disagreement is recorded in the logbook with a one-sentence rationale; nothing is silently rejected.
