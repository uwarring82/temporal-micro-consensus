## Summary

A comprehensive Rev. Mod. Phys. review of optical atomic clocks — frequency standards based on narrow optical transitions in trapped single ions and in neutral-atom ensembles (optical lattices). It covers the ingredients of a clock (a stable local oscillator locked to an atomic resonance, with cycle counting defining elapsed time), the stability and systematic-uncertainty budgets of state-of-the-art clocks (then approaching 10⁻¹⁸), and the experimental techniques. For this framework the load-bearing parts are the conceptual framing of what a clock measures, the treatment of relativistic frequency shifts, and the applications (atomic-time coordination, relativistic geodesy, fundamental-constant variation).

## Key points

- Clock operating principle (Sec. I): a clock needs a regular periodic event at a stable reference frequency; counting cycles produces time intervals — Maxwell's idea that atoms are "natural standards," identical and reproducible, underlies all atomic frequency standards.
- Stability quantified by the Allan variance σ_y²(τ); systematic shifts characterized as fractional frequency errors Δf/f₀ that must be calibrated/corrected, with errors split into statistical (averaged down with τ) and systematic types.
- Relativistic shifts (Sec. II.C.2): first-order Doppler (largest for single-photon transitions; motivates confined atoms), second-order Doppler = time dilation ((v²/2c²)), and gravitational redshift δf/f₀ = gΔh/c² (~10⁻¹⁷ per 10 cm height) — explicitly framed as the atoms/lasers/clocks being in different reference frames whose proper times differ.
- Worldwide atomic time (Sec. VII.A): TAI/UTC built by BIPM by averaging ~400 microwave clocks; optical standards now surpass Cs and are recommended as "secondary representations of the second"; emphasizes that disseminating a shared time scale requires comparison/synchronization across many clocks (time transfer).
- Applications: relativistic ("chronometric") geodesy — clocks as gravity-potential sensors; optical clocks in space for tests of LLI/LPI and the gravitational redshift; clock comparisons constraining temporal variation of fundamental constants (α, μ) via species-dependent sensitivity coefficients K.

## Relevance to the framework

This is the empirical/operational backbone for Cluster C and a key reference for Claim II and Claim V. The review makes concrete that a usable, classical time variable is something *built by consensus across many clocks*: TAI/UTC is literally a redundancy-weighted average over a large ensemble of frequency standards, and time transfer / inter-clock comparison is what turns individual oscillators into a shared time scale — a direct physical instance of the framework's "temporal inference becomes redundant, stable, compressible" criterion. The relativistic-shift section grounds Claim I (no single sharp proper time: each clock's rate depends on its frame, velocity, and gravitational potential) and the cross-probe-mismatch / robustness anchors of Claim IV (systematic-shift budgets are exactly catalogues of when different physical records disagree). Fundamental-constant-variation tests show clocks operationally probing whether the "ticking rate" itself is stable.
