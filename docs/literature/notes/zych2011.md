## Summary
The authors propose a Mach–Zehnder interferometer in Earth's gravitational field using a particle that carries an evolving internal "clock" degree of freedom. Because proper time runs at different rates along the two arms, the clock evolves into distinguishable states, and quantum complementarity converts this which-path information into a *loss of interferometric visibility* — a gravitationally induced decoherence distinct from the usual Aharonov–Bohm-type phase shift. They argue this would be the first unambiguous test of the genuinely general-relativistic notion of proper time in quantum mechanics.

## Key points
- Without a clock, only a trajectory-dependent phase appears, always reinterpretable as an AB phase in flat space-time; the *visibility drop* is what cannot be so reinterpreted and requires proper time to have operational meaning.
- Visibility 𝒱 = |⟨τ₁|τ₂⟩| equals the overlap of the clock states on the two paths; complementarity gives 𝒱² + 𝒟² = 1 with 𝒟 the which-path distinguishability (Eq. 5).
- The internal energy couples directly to the gravitational potential in the effective Hamiltonian (Eq. 7), so the clock literally ticks at different rates on the two arms; 𝒱 = |cos(ΔE ΔV ΔT / 2ℏc²)| (Eq. 13).
- Visibility can be written purely via the orthogonalization (Mandelstam–Tamm) time t⊥ and the proper-time difference: 𝒱 = |cos((Δτ/t⊥)(π/2))| (Eq. 16) — a clean Fisher/time-energy connection.
