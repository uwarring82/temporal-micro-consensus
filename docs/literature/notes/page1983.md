## Summary
The founding paper of the relational ("conditional probability") approach to time in quantum mechanics. Arguing that an energy superselection rule should hold for a closed system (the Universe) just as a charge superselection rule does, the authors conclude that the global state must be stationary and coordinate time t is fundamentally unobservable. They show that the *appearance* of dynamics can nonetheless be recovered entirely from stationary observables, expressed as conditional probabilities relating a subsystem chosen as a "clock" to the rest of the system.

## Key points
- Toy model: n spin-j particles precessing about z. One particle's spin direction is read as a clock; conditional probabilities of the others' spins given the clock reading reproduce ordinary Heisenberg evolution — with no reference to external t.
- Time-averaged ("smeared") projection operators P̄ are stationary and hence count as legitimate observables; conditional expectation E(A|τ) = tr(P̄_τ A P̄_τ ρ)/tr(P̄_τ ρ) carries the dynamics.
- Demonstrates that the clock-conditioned expectation evolves per the Heisenberg equation of motion for the rest of the system, given a non-interacting clock+rest split H = H_c + H_r.
- Notes a precision limit: resolving fine time intervals requires large energy exchange, so any real clock has finite accuracy (cites DeWitt-type bounds) — an early statement of a time–energy tradeoff.
- Explicitly anchors time as an *internal* clock reading, not an external parameter; "time is defined so that motion looks simple" is treated as a useful but non-fundamental idealization.
