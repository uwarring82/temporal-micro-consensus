## Summary

The full-length follow-up to riedel2010, extending the scattered-photon model of quantum Darwinism to blackbody sources covering an arbitrary section of the sky and to more general initial states of the object. It separates the two ways the photon environment can be mixed and shows which one matters for record-keeping: directional (angular) mixedness of the illumination reduces the environment's ability to *record* the object's position, whereas energy-spectrum mixedness does not, and decoherence proceeds regardless.

## Key points

- Computes object–photon mutual information and redundancy for blackbody illumination subtending an arbitrary solid angle, interpolating between the point-source (high redundancy) and isotropic (low redundancy) limits of the 2010 Letter.
- Distinguishes two mixing types: mixing of the angular (record-carrying) modes decreases redundancy, while mixing of the energy spectrum only changes which modes resolve the object — confirming earlier abstract results that initial environment mixedness slows but does not stop record production.
- Decoherence rate and redundancy rate have different origins/dependences; the environment can decohere the system far faster than it builds redundant records (the §2.4 "origin of the difference" analysis).
- Results extended to general superpositions, including "M-way-cat" states, showing the qualitative mutual-information / redundancy-plateau behaviour is robust.
- Reinforces that everyday light (the dominant channel by which observers gather information) supports objective classical position records.

## Relevance to the framework

The explicit decoupling of *decoherence rate* from *redundancy/record-keeping rate* is the most framework-relevant result: it operationalizes Claim III's demand to discriminate generic decoherence from the failure of a usable (temporal) consensus record. For temporal records, the analogous statement is that a subsystem can decohere without its evolution becoming redundantly/inferentially recorded — i.e., decoherence alone does not certify a classical proper-time variable. The "record-carrying vs irrelevant modes" decomposition supports Claim IV's robustness-under-coarse-graining and cross-probe-mismatch anchors.
