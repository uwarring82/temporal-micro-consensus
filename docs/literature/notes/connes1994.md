## Summary
Connes and Rovelli propose the *thermal time hypothesis*: in a generally covariant quantum theory there is no preferred external time, and the physical flow of time is not fundamental but is determined by the thermodynamical (statistical) state of the system. Mathematically they identify physical time with the Tomita–Takesaki modular flow σ_t^φ of a state φ on the observable von Neumann algebra — a state-dependent one-parameter automorphism group. The co-cycle Radon–Nikodym theorem makes the *outer* part of this flow state-independent and intrinsic to the algebra, giving a canonical, abstract notion of time generated by the non-commutativity of the (type III) algebra itself.

## Key points
- In conventional QM the Gibbs/KMS state ρ = exp(−βH) already encodes the time flow in H; Connes-Rovelli invert this, *postulating* that for a generally covariant system the modular (thermal) flow α_t^ρ of the state ρ *defines* physical time.
- In the non-relativistic limit, a KMS thermal state |ω⟩ reproduces the ordinary Hamiltonian time flow; the classical limit recovers Hamilton's equations and the Gibbs postulate from the modular flow relation.
- Time becomes macroscopic and thermodynamical in origin — tied to coarse-graining / ignorance of innumerable microscopic DOF; a system with many DOF described by its statistical state has a flow that "coincides with what we perceive as the physical flow of time."
- Cosmological support: the modular/thermal time defined by the cosmic microwave background coincides with conventional Friedmann–Robertson–Walker time — cited as the strongest evidence.
- Unifies puzzling facts (Unruh temperature, Hawking radiation, Rindler-wedge restriction) as restrictions of the observable algebra to subalgebras giving different time flows; authors stress the hypothesis is tentative and somewhat vague but connects a striking number of facts.

## Relevance to the framework
**Cluster G · Tier 3** background/positioning reference. It is the deepest precedent for the framework's core thesis that classical time is *emergent and not microscopically fundamental* (**Claim I**) and that its emergence is statistical/thermodynamical, i.e. tied to coarse-graining over many DOF (**Claim II**, robustness-under-coarse-graining of **Claim IV**). The state-dependence and the idea that restricting to different observable subalgebras yields different (or no) time flow prefigures the framework's notion that a subsystem can *fail* to support a classical temporal-consensus variable (**Claim III**). Conceptually orthogonal in mechanism (modular flow / operator algebras vs redundancy of physical records) but strongly aligned in spirit; useful for **Claim V** positioning as an alternative emergent-time program that is *not* multipartite-redundancy-based.
