## Summary

A proof-of-principle matter-wave experiment in which a single atomic "clock" (the internal Zeeman-spin state of a ⁸⁷Rb BEC) is coherently split into two spatial wave packets that are then recombined to interfere. By applying a magnetic-gradient pulse the authors make the internal clock "tick" at different rates along the two arms, simulating a differential proper-time lag. When the two clock readings become orthogonal, the spatial interference visibility drops to zero — the clock has recorded "which path" — whereas equal ticking restores full visibility. This demonstrates that a clock degree of freedom acts as a which-path witness, with visibility equal to the overlap (scalar product) of the two clock states.

## Key points

- The clock is a two-level internal system (m_F = 1, 2 of the F = 2 manifold) prepared in (|1⟩+|2⟩)/√2; its Bloch-vector orientation is the "clock hand."
- A Stern-Gerlach-type chip-based interferometer splits the BEC spatially along gravity (z); a magnetic gradient ∂B/∂z for time T_G induces a controllable *differential* clock rotation between the two arms.
- Key result: interference visibility = |⟨clock_upper|clock_lower⟩|; orthogonal clock states (relative rotation π) erase the fringes (which-path info recorded), parallel clock states (2π) restore them — visibility oscillates with T_G.
- Control experiments: a single-internal-state ("no clock") interferometer shows no such visibility oscillation; modulating clock preparation (RF duration T_R) confirms the effect is genuinely due to the clock reading, not the gradient pulse alone.
- Connects Bohr–Einstein clock-in-a-box debate to a concrete apparatus; differential clock rates are not yet at the level of real gravitational time dilation but the principle is established.
