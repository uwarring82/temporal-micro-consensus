## Summary
Rovelli argues that the "measurement problem" arises from an incorrect tacit assumption — the notion of an *observer-independent state* and observer-independent values of physical quantities — analogous to how absolute simultaneity bedevilled pre-1905 electrodynamics. He proposes that quantum states and the values of physical quantities are always *relative to another physical system* (an "observer," with no privileged observer/observed distinction); the theory describes only the information that systems have about each other, and is complete. He further suggests deriving the QM formalism from simple information-theoretic postulates (a system carries a limited amount of information, yet more relevant information is always obtainable).

## Key points
- Central thesis (Sec. 2.3): the quantum state of S "exists only if some system O is describing it"; a quantity's value v is always v *with respect to* a given observer — there is "no description of the universe in toto, only a quantum-interrelated net of partial descriptions."
- All physical systems are equivalent ("golden rule") — observers are themselves quantum systems; two observers may give different but equally valid accounts of the same sequence of events.
- Decoherence is explicitly invoked (Zurek 1981; Joos–Zeh 1985) as the physical mechanism that "saves the phenomena" and makes the relational/perspectival values effectively classical; consistent/decoherent-histories interpretations are read as confirming (not refuting) the relational conclusion.
- Information-theoretic reconstruction program: Shannon-style information is the basic notion; "correlations between the properties of quantum systems are more basic than the properties themselves" — Rovelli notes Zurek (1982) reached identical conclusions.
- Time/simultaneity analogy is structural: just as simultaneity became frame-relative, here state and value become observer-relative; "which branch" / "which observer" is treated as a new dimension of indexicality alongside "which time."
