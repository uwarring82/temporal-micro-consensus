## Summary

Takes quantum Darwinism from abstract spin/oscillator toy models to a physically realistic setting: a small dielectric sphere in a position superposition illuminated by blackbody radiation. The authors compute the quantum mutual information between the object and fragments of its scattered-photon environment and show that information about the object's position proliferates extremely fast and to enormous redundancy — orders of magnitude beyond previous solvable models — confirming that everyday objects acquire robustly objective, classical positions through ordinary light scattering.

## Key points

- Models collisional decoherence of a sphere (radius a, permittivity ε) by point-source vs isotropic blackbody photons; decoherence time τ_D ∝ c⁶ℏ⁶/(a⁶Δx²T⁵), generally extremely short.
- Mutual information I(S:F_f) vs fragment fraction f develops the characteristic redundancy plateau at H_S = ln2 for t ≫ τ_D (point-source illumination); the first few photons reveal almost everything, the rest are redundant.
- Redundancy R_δ ≈ (t/τ_D)/ln[(2δln2)⁻¹] grows *linearly in time* and depends only logarithmically (weakly) on the information deficit δ.
- Quantitative example: a 1 µm dust grain illuminated by sunlight for 1 µs imprints its location ~10⁸ times in scattered photons — "huge" redundancy.
- Isotropic (fully directionally mixed) illumination yields essentially zero redundancy — the photon directional states are already saturated and cannot store new information; real (non-uniform) illumination loses only an O(1) factor.

## Relevance to the framework

A concrete demonstration that the redundancy/mutual-information machinery (Claim II, IV) holds for real macroscopic systems and ordinary environments, supporting the framework's bid to apply the same architecture to temporal records in laboratory settings. The linear-in-time growth of redundancy is suggestive for *temporal* records: the longer the elapsed evolution, the more redundant the proper-time imprint. The isotropic-illumination null result is a clean example of Claim III structural failure (a channel that decoheres but stores no usable record), useful for distinguishing decoherence from loss of the consensus variable.
